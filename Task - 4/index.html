About Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam

Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, also known as the "Missile Man of India," was an Indian aerospace scientist who served as the 11th President of India from 2002 to 2007. His work in the field of science and technology, especially at ISRO and DRDO, contributed significantly to India's defense and space programs.

Born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, Dr. Kalam came from a humble background. Despite facing numerous challenges in his early life, he never gave up on his dreams and became one of the most respected leaders in India. He played a pivotal role in the development of India's missile and nuclear weapons programs, earning him the nickname "Missile Man."

Beyond his contributions to science, Dr. Kalam was known for his humility, inspirational speeches, and his vision to see India as a developed nation. He was a true people's president who inspired millions of young Indians to dream big and achieve their goals. His legacy continues to inspire people around the world.

Biography

Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam's life journey is a true reflection of hard work, dedication, and a passion for learning. Born into a modest Tamil Muslim family in Rameswaram, he had a tough childhood marked by financial difficulties. Despite this, his hunger for knowledge and understanding of the world led him to pursue a career in aeronautical engineering at Madras Institute of Technology (MIT).

After graduating, he joined the Defense Research and Development Organization (DRDO) and later the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO), where he was a part of several successful satellite launch missions. He was also a key figure in India's nuclear tests at Pokhran in 1998, which made India a nuclear-armed nation.

In 2002, Dr. Kalam became the President of India, serving until 2007. During his presidency, he was known for his approachable nature and his efforts to reach out to the common man, especially students. He continued his interaction with the youth until his last breath on July 27, 2015, when he collapsed while delivering a lecture at the Indian Institute of Management, Shillong.
